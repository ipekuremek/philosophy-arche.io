A short explanation about Arche

To understand the philosophers of the pre-Socratic era, a crucial concept is necessary: Arche. Understanding Arche may require a bit of background in Ancient Greek thinking. In Ancient Greece, there was a belief that nothing could come into existence from nothing. Arche, in simple terms, could be thought of as the substance that has always been around, not created from nothing; a definition of it can be ‘Principle' or the “source of action”. The era of Naturalist and Pluralist philosophy unfolded in the pursuit of the "arche," leading to significant revelations. Thales, mentioned as the first philosopher, identified the arche as the element of water. According to him, everything originated from water. Anaximenes, on the other hand, attributed the arche to the element of air. The philosopher of the Pluralist era, Democritus, referring to the arche as "atom," represents a crucial scientific achievement. According to him, the atom is the smallest and indivisible unit. The inability of our senses to perceive the atom has, in fact, given rise to the concept of skepticism. According to him, our inability to grasp the arche - the atom - through our senses underscores the limitations of our senses in acquiring knowledge. The philosopher Heraclitus, renowned for his philosophy of change, defines the arche as fire. Fire, according to him, is the greatest transformer. It transforms whatever comes into contact with it. Therefore, according to him, the arche must be fire. Regarding the concept of the "arche," nearly all Pre-Socratic era ancient philosophers have some idea, and the distinction between Naturalist and Pluralist philosophy emerges here. The philosophers we call Naturalists have a monist perspective and define the arche with just one element or concept. As for Pluralist philosophers, we observe that they now define the arche as the coming together of multiple elements or substances with the most general definition. In the case of Empedocles, the arche includes the four elements, but this is insufficient for the formation of "things"; according to him, there must be a propelling/unifying force bringing these "things" together: Love. Conversely, Hate has a destructive and divisive effect. In the case of Anaxagoras, the arche is the "spermata" or seeds. Everything has its unique seed, and if apple seeds are abundant in something, that thing becomes an apple; if there are many gold seeds, it becomes gold.
